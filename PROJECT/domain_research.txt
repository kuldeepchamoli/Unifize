PART 1: WHAT UNIFIZE SELLS & WHO USES IT
=====================================

Unifize is a cloud-based Quality Management System (QMS) platform built for
manufacturing companies — especially those in regulated industries like medical
devices, pharma, aerospace, and automotive.

Their core product modules:
  - QMS (Quality Management System): Central hub for managing non-conformances,
    CAPAs, deviations, change controls, and audit findings — all as linked records
    with automated workflows.
  - Complaints Management: Intake, triage, investigate, and resolve customer/internal
    complaints. Links each complaint to root cause analysis and CAPA actions.
  - Document Control: Version-controlled SOPs, work instructions, and specs with
    approval workflows. Ensures everyone works off the latest revision.
  - Audit Trail: Automatic, tamper-proof log of every action taken on every record —
    who did what, when, and why. Required for FDA compliance.

Who uses it:
  - Quality engineers, quality managers, regulatory affairs teams
  - Manufacturing/production supervisors
  - Supplier quality engineers
  - Companies that must comply with FDA 21 CFR Part 11, ISO 9001, ISO 13485,
    AS9100, or IATF 16949

PART 2: 20 DOMAIN TERMS — DEFINED
=====================================

1.  NC (Non-Conformance)
    A product, process, or material that does not meet its specified requirements.
    Example from your data: "steel fasteners failed tensile strength testing — 23% below spec"

2.  CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action)
    Corrective = fix the problem that already happened.
    Preventive = stop it from happening again.
    The formal process: identify issue -> root cause analysis -> implement fix -> verify effectiveness.

3.  Deviation
    An authorized or unauthorized departure from an approved procedure or specification.
    Example: using a temperature 5 degrees outside the validated range during production.

4.  Root Cause Analysis (RCA)
    Systematic investigation to find the fundamental reason a problem occurred.
    Common methods: 5 Whys, Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagram, Fault Tree Analysis.

5.  SCAR (Supplier Corrective Action Request)
    Formal request sent to a supplier when their material/component fails to meet spec.
    Example from your data: "Issued SCAR to supplier. Quarantined remaining lot."

6.  CAR (Corrective Action Request)
    Similar to SCAR but can be internal or external. A formal document requesting
    that a problem be investigated and corrected.

7.  MRB (Material Review Board)
    A cross-functional team that decides the disposition of non-conforming material:
    use-as-is, rework, scrap, or return to supplier.

8.  Lot / Batch
    A defined quantity of material produced under uniform conditions. Traceability
    requires tracking which lot went into which product.
    Example: "Supplier lot #BT-4421"

9.  Quarantine
    Physically or logically isolating suspect material so it cannot be used until
    a disposition decision is made.

10. Disposition
    The decision on what to do with non-conforming material: scrap, rework,
    use-as-is (with justification), or return to vendor.

11. Audit Trail
    An unbroken, chronological record of every action on a quality record.
    Required by 21 CFR Part 11 — must capture who, what, when, and why.

12. SOP (Standard Operating Procedure)
    Step-by-step documented instructions for performing a routine activity.
    Document control ensures only the current approved version is in use.

13. 21 CFR Part 11
    US FDA regulation governing electronic records and electronic signatures.
    Key requirements:
    - Audit trails that cannot be altered
    - Access controls (user authentication)
    - Electronic signatures must be legally equivalent to handwritten ones
    - System validation
    Applies to: any FDA-regulated company using electronic records (pharma,
    medical devices, food, biotech).

14. ISO 9001
    International standard for Quality Management Systems. Requires organizations
    to have processes for handling non-conformances, taking corrective actions,
    and continuously improving. CAPA is central to ISO 9001 compliance.

15. ISO 13485
    Like ISO 9001 but specifically for medical device manufacturers.
    Stricter requirements for traceability, complaint handling, and risk management.

16. Cpk (Process Capability Index)
    Statistical measure of how well a process stays within spec limits.
    Cpk > 1.33 = capable process. Cpk < 1.0 = process is producing defects.
    Example from your data: "Required process capability study (Cpk > 1.33)"

17. PPAP (Production Part Approval Process)
    Formal process (common in automotive/aerospace) where a supplier demonstrates
    they can consistently produce parts that meet all requirements.
    Example from your data: "Submitted PPAP requirement for all passive components"

18. Incoming Inspection / Incoming QC
    Testing or examining purchased materials upon receipt, before they enter production.
    Example: "Incoming tensile test," "visual inspection at dock," "sieve test"

19. Change Control
    Formal process to evaluate, approve, and document any change to a product,
    process, or document. Prevents uncontrolled changes that could affect quality.

20. Effectiveness Verification
    After implementing a CAPA, you must verify it actually worked — did the problem
    stop recurring? Typically checked 30-90 days after implementation.

PART 3: 3 USER PAIN POINTS (IN MY OWN WORDS)
=====================================

1. FINDING PAST COMPLAINTS IS A NIGHTMARE
   Quality engineers deal with hundreds or thousands of complaint records over time.
   When a new complaint comes in, the first question is always: "Have we seen this
   before?" But complaints are described in free text, using inconsistent terminology
   (one person writes "cracking," another writes "fracture," another writes "surface
   failure"). Keyword search fails. So engineers waste hours manually searching, or
   they miss the connection entirely — and reinvestigate a problem that was already
   solved. This is the #1 pain point a RAG system can address.

2. ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS IS REPETITIVE BUT NOT STANDARDIZED
   Many complaints have similar root causes (supplier quality, process drift, design
   tolerance), but each engineer writes their RCA differently. There's no easy way
   to say "show me all complaints where the root cause was similar to this one" or
   "what CAPA worked last time for this type of supplier defect?" The institutional
   knowledge lives in people's heads or buried in PDFs.

3. COMPLIANCE DOCUMENTATION IS TIME-CONSUMING AND HIGH-STAKES
   Every complaint must be documented with: what happened, investigation steps, root
   cause, CAPA taken, and effectiveness verification. If an FDA auditor pulls a
   complaint record and any of these fields are incomplete, it's a finding — possibly
   a warning letter. Quality teams spend enormous time on documentation to avoid
   regulatory risk, when they'd rather spend that time actually solving problems.

PART 4: FDA WARNING LETTER EXAMPLES (COMPLAINT HANDLING FAILURES)
=====================================

These are real, publicly documented FDA enforcement cases:

Example 1: Abbott / St. Jude Medical (2017) — Cardiac Devices
  Violation: Failure to investigate complaints about device malfunctions and patient
  injuries per 21 CFR 820.198 (Complaint Files). Complaints involving serious device
  failures were closed without adequate investigation. Medical Device Reports (MDRs)
  were not filed for reportable adverse events.
  What went wrong: Complaints were classified in ways that avoided triggering
  mandatory FDA reporting. No root cause analysis performed on device malfunction
  reports.

Example 2: Philips Respironics (2021-2022) — CPAP / Ventilator Machines
  Violation: Thousands of complaints over several years about foam degradation and
  black particles in CPAP machines — but failed to escalate or act on them timely.
  Cited under 21 CFR 820.198. Inadequate CAPA despite a known recurring problem.
  Ultimately led to a massive Class I recall affecting millions of devices.
  What went wrong: Complaint trend data was available for years but the system
  failed to flag the pattern. This is the textbook case for why complaint retrieval
  and trend analysis matter — the signal was in the data, but no one connected it.

Example 3: Ranbaxy Laboratories (2008-2014) — Pharmaceutical Manufacturing
  Violation: Multiple warning letters + consent decree. Failure to investigate
  out-of-specification (OOS) results per 21 CFR 211.192. CAPA records were either
  absent or did not address root causes. Batch records and stability data were
  fabricated. Company paid a $500 million settlement.
  What went wrong: Systemic quality failure. Complaints and OOS findings were
  ignored or "tested into compliance" (retesting until a passing result was obtained).

Common failure patterns across FDA warning letters:
  - Complaints closed without investigation (21 CFR 820.198)
  - No root cause analysis performed (21 CFR 820.100)
  - Failure to identify trends from complaint data (21 CFR 820.198(a))
  - CAPA actions not verified for effectiveness (21 CFR 820.100(a))
  - Adverse events not reported to FDA (21 CFR 803)


=====================================
PART 5: WHY RETRIEVAL MATTERS FOR THIS DOMAIN
=====================================

In manufacturing quality management, retrieval matters because every new complaint
could be a repeat of a solved problem or an early signal of a systemic failure —
but finding that connection across thousands of free-text records with inconsistent
terminology is exactly what keyword search fails at and semantic retrieval excels at.
