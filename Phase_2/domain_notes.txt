SECTION 1: QMS DOMAIN TERMS (20 terms with plain-English definitions)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. NCR (Non-Conformance Report)
   A formal record created when a product, process, or service fails to meet
   a specified requirement. Think of it as a "something went wrong" ticket
   that must be investigated and closed with evidence.

2. CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action)
   A two-part process: Corrective Action fixes the root cause of an existing
   problem; Preventive Action stops a similar problem from happening in the
   future. CAPA is the backbone of continuous improvement in regulated
   industries.

3. Deviation
   Any departure from an approved procedure, specification, or standard.
   Deviations can be planned (intentional, pre-approved) or unplanned
   (unexpected events that need investigation).

4. Root Cause Analysis (RCA)
   A systematic method (e.g., 5 Whys, Fishbone/Ishikawa diagram) used to
   identify the fundamental reason a problem occurred, rather than just
   treating symptoms.

5. Lot / Batch
   A defined quantity of product manufactured under uniform conditions.
   Lot traceability is critical — if a complaint comes in, you need to know
   exactly which lot was affected and where every unit from that lot went.

6. Supplier Corrective Action Request (SCAR)
   A formal request sent to a supplier when their material or component
   causes a quality issue. The supplier must investigate, identify root cause,
   and provide evidence of corrective action.

7. Complaint (per FDA 21 CFR 820.198)
   Any written, electronic, or oral communication that alleges deficiencies
   related to the identity, quality, durability, reliability, safety,
   effectiveness, or performance of a device after it is released for
   distribution. Every complaint must be evaluated; some trigger MDRs.

8. MDR (Medical Device Report)
   A mandatory report filed with the FDA when a device may have caused or
   contributed to a death or serious injury, or has malfunctioned in a way
   that could cause harm if the malfunction recurred. Strict timelines apply
   (30 days, or 5 days for emergencies).

9. 21 CFR Part 11
   FDA regulation governing electronic records and electronic signatures.
   Requires audit trails, access controls, and validation of computer systems
   used in regulated processes. Unifize specifically markets compliance with
   this regulation.

10. Audit Trail
    A secure, time-stamped, unalterable log of who did what, when, and why
    within a system. Required by 21 CFR Part 11. Any change to a regulated
    record must be traceable.

11. DHR (Device History Record)
    A compilation of records containing the complete production history of
    a finished device — proves that the device was manufactured according
    to the approved Device Master Record (DMR).

12. DMR (Device Master Record)
    The collection of records that defines the finished device: specifications,
    production processes, quality procedures, labeling, and packaging.

13. Design Control
    A systematic set of practices and procedures (per 21 CFR 820.30) used
    during product development to ensure design outputs meet design inputs.
    Includes design reviews, verification, validation, and transfer.

14. Validation
    Documented evidence that a process, system, or method consistently
    produces a result meeting predetermined specifications. Process validation,
    software validation, and cleaning validation are common types.

15. OOS (Out of Specification)
    A test result that falls outside the accepted criteria. OOS results
    trigger investigations — you cannot simply re-test until you get a
    passing result.

16. FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis)
    A proactive risk assessment tool that identifies potential failure modes
    in a process or product, rates their severity, occurrence, and
    detectability, and prioritizes actions to reduce risk.

17. QMS (Quality Management System)
    The overarching framework of policies, processes, procedures, and records
    that an organization uses to ensure its products and services consistently
    meet customer and regulatory requirements. ISO 13485 is the QMS standard
    for medical devices.

18. 483 Observation
    A form (FDA Form 483) issued by an FDA inspector at the end of a facility
    inspection listing observed conditions that may violate the FD&C Act.
    Companies must respond with corrective actions. Repeated 483s can lead
    to Warning Letters.

19. Warning Letter
    A formal FDA communication stating that a company is in significant
    violation of regulations. More serious than a 483 — public, and failure
    to respond adequately can lead to consent decrees, injunctions, or
    import alerts.

20. Change Control
    A formal process for proposing, reviewing, approving, and documenting
    changes to validated processes, equipment, materials, or documents.
    Prevents uncontrolled changes that could introduce quality risks.

SECTION 2: USER PAIN POINTS (from G2/Capterra review themes)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Pain Point 1: Complaint records buried across disconnected systems
   Users repeatedly mention that before adopting a platform like Unifize,
   complaint data lived in spreadsheets, email threads, and paper forms.
   Finding the full history of a complaint — from intake to CAPA closure —
   required manually stitching together information from 3-4 systems. This
   slows down FDA response timelines and increases the risk of missing a
   reportable event.

Pain Point 2: Lack of real-time visibility into open CAPAs and their status
   Quality managers struggle to answer "how many CAPAs are overdue right now?"
   without running manual reports. Dashboards and real-time tracking are
   cited as major reasons for switching to a digital QMS. When CAPAs slip,
   audit findings pile up.

Pain Point 3: Audit trail gaps and 21 CFR Part 11 compliance anxiety
   Smaller medical device companies especially worry about whether their
   current tools (SharePoint, Google Docs, Excel) can withstand an FDA
   inspection. The inability to prove who changed what, when, and why
   is a recurring source of stress. Users value Unifize's built-in audit
   trail and e-signature capabilities because they reduce the burden of
   proving compliance.

SECTION 3: WHY RETRIEVAL QUALITY MATTERS FOR REGULATED COMPLAINT RECORDS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In regulated industries, a missed or incorrectly retrieved complaint record
can directly lead to patient harm (unreported MDRs), regulatory action
(FDA Warning Letters citing incomplete complaint files under 21 CFR 820.198),
and legal liability — making retrieval precision and recall not just a
software quality metric but a patient safety and compliance requirement
where errors carry consequences measured in human health outcomes, not just
user inconvenience.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: KEY REGULATORY REFERENCES CONSULTED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- 21 CFR 820.198 — Complaint Files
  * Requires manufacturers to maintain complaint files and document
    investigations. If an investigation is not performed, the reason must be
    documented. Complaints involving possible failure of a device to meet
    specifications must be reviewed, evaluated, and investigated.

- FDA Form 483 Observations (complaint-related examples):
  * Common observation: "Complaints were not reviewed, evaluated, and
    investigated as required" — indicates companies failing to process
    all incoming complaints.
  * Common observation: "No written procedures for receiving, reviewing, and
    evaluating complaints" — basic procedural gap.
  * Common observation: "Failure to identify complaints that represent
    events which must be reported to FDA under MDR requirements" — missing
    the escalation from complaint to MDR.

- FDA Warning Letter themes (complaint-related):
  * Failure to maintain complete complaint records.
  * Failure to investigate complaints involving possible device failures.
  * Failure to identify trends in complaint data.
  * Inadequate CAPA effectiveness checks after complaint investigations.
